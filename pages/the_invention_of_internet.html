The Invention of Internet

The internet was invented by the combined work of a myriad of scientists, engineers, and programmer. These individuals are credited for developing new features and technologies that were eventually merged to create the internet.

In the early 1960’s J.C.R Licklider, a computer scientist and a team lead at Advanced Research Projects Agency (ARPA) had a seminal idea of an “Intergalactic Network” of computers. His idea shortly led to the developed concept of “packet switching”. Packet switching is a mode of data transmission in which a message is broken into several parts which are sent independently, over whatever route is optimum for each packet, and reassembled at the destination. Packet switching became a major component of the internet. Licklider, encouraged research into time sharing and helped in creating and laying out the foundation for computer networking and ARPANET. ARPANET was the first workable prototype of the internet. ARPANET used packet switching so that multiple computers can communicate on a single network.

ARPAnet delivered its first node-to-node communication message from one computer to another. The ARPA network ended up crashing but hope was not lost. Scientists Robert Kahn and Vinton Cerf developed Transmission Control Protocol and Internet Protocol (TCP/IP), a communication model that set rules that governs how data can be transmitted between multiple networks. ARPAnet integrated TCP/IP and soon researchers constructed the “networks of networks” that became the Internet. Computer scientist Tim Berners-Lee invented the World Wide Web, the means of accessing data online in the form of websites and hyperlinks. The web popularized the internet among the public rather than a government privilege.
